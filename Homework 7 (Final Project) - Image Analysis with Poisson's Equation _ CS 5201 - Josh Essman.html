Background

Computer vision is a field that focuses on the computational extraction of information from digital images. Applications of computer vision are virtually limitless and range from obstacle recognition for autonomous vehicles to camera tracking and facial authentication on the latest smartphones.

This assignment will focus on extracting information from a simplified image - a silhouette. Given a silhouette representation of a shape, it is often desirable to obtain information on certain properties of the shape, for example:

  • The shapes of components (if the shape is composite)
  • The aspect ratio of the shape
  • The degree of similarity w.r.t. another shape
  • The orientation of the shape (vertical, horizontal, diagonal)
  • The edges of the shape that are concave or convex

The above information is particularly useful when one wishes to identify a shape. A simple example might be a circle. In the context of the above criteria, one could describe a circle as a shape that satisfies the following:

  • Has a 1:1 aspect ratio
  • Its orientation does not change if the silhouette is rotated about the center of the shape
  • It has no concave edges, only convex edges

Shape identification has several important applications. For example, shape identification techniques very similar to the one you will implement have been used to identify abnormalities in the shapes of different components of the brain (using a digital MRI).

The above properties can be determined by determining the distance from a given point on the shape (in the context of an image, this would be a pixel) to the edge of the shape. For a circle of radius 1, a point at the center of the circle would have a distance value of 1, and a point at the edge of the circle would have a distance value of 0. For a circle with known radius, determining the distance to the edge is simple. But this problem becomes significantly more complicated for polygons, and even more so for irregular shapes.

We are able to characterize this problem in the general case as a differential equation. Throughout the semester you have solved differential equations with a single dependent variable (ODEs), and now you will solve a multivariate differential equation (PDE) using the techniques discussed in lecture.

In the context of this application, an image can be thought of as a 2-dimensional array of color values.

The seminal paper that guides this project is Shape Representation and Classification Using the Poisson Equation by Gorelick, et al.

Program Specification

You will solve the following equation:

∂2U∂x2+∂2U∂y2=f(x,y)∂2U∂x2+∂2U∂y2=f(x,y)

This equation is sometimes written using the following alternate forms:

ΔU=f(x,y)ΔU=f(x,y)
∇2U=f(x,y)∇2U=f(x,y)
Uxx+Uyy=f(x,y)Uxx+Uyy=f(x,y)

This is called Poisson’s equation.

For our silhouette processing application, we set f(x,y)=1f(x,y)=1. Technically, we set f(x,y)=−4h2f(x,y)=−4h2, such that the term obtained in lecture cancels to 11.

In the case of a silhouette (a black shape on a white background), your boundary is the parts of the image that are white, for which you can assume U=0U=0. You should also treat the edges of the image itself as boundaries where U=0U=0. This only becomes a concern for silhouettes that do not have a white border around them.

You are responsible for finding the values of UU for each black pixel in the image.
